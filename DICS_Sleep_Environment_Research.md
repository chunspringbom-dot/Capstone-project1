# The Impact of Sleep Environment on the Sleep Quality of DICS Students

**Grade 10 — Chanyoung Kang**

---

## Motivation

This project began with a simple question: *Why do students at our school fall asleep so often during class?* Students at regular high schools push through on just five hours of sleep and still study hard — so why are our students dozing off even after getting a seemingly sufficient eight hours? I also wanted to examine whether the wealth of health information reported in the news is actually trustworthy.

---

## Project Goal

This project aims to help DICS students achieve better sleep quality within the same amount of sleep time — reducing in-class drowsiness and ultimately improving overall quality of life. The data collected is still in an early stage with many variables and limitations. However, as more data accumulates, the goal is to build a **personalized app** that identifies each individual's optimal sleep conditions.

---

## Hypothesis

- **Darkness and quiet** will lead to higher sleep satisfaction and fewer nighttime awakenings.
  - Rationale: News reports indicate that light prevents the brain from falling asleep, and noise is known to reduce sleep quality.
- **Around 8 hours** of sleep is optimal; too much or too little will leave a person more tired.
- **Not eating for at least 2 hours** before bed will improve sleep quality.

---

## Research Questions

1. Does the presence of light affect sleep satisfaction?
2. Does noise affect the number of awakenings during sleep?
3. What range of sleep duration is most efficient?
4. Do eating and exercise before bedtime affect sleep quality?

---

## Methodology

**Independent variables (environmental factors):**
- Presence or absence of light
- Level of noise
- Whether exercise was performed within 2 hours of bedtime
- Whether food was consumed before bedtime
- Total sleep duration

**Dependent variables (outcome measures):**
- Time taken to fall asleep
- Number of awakenings during the night
- Number of naps taken the following day
- Subjective sleep satisfaction rating
- How the participant felt upon waking in the morning

A composite **sleep score** was calculated from these measures. A total of **32 data points** were collected and visualized in charts.

## Data Results & Anomalies

Many results contradicted the initial hypotheses.

### Light
The hypothesis that *darker conditions lead to better sleep* was **not supported**. Surprisingly, participants who slept with a mood lamp on reported **higher sleep scores** than those who slept in complete darkness.

**Follow-up finding:** When light and noise data were analyzed together, it was observed that in dark conditions, people were more likely to talk loudly, whereas mood-lamp environments tended to be quieter. This suggests the noise variable was a **confounding factor**.

### Food Intake Before Bed
The hypothesis that heavy snacks (e.g., ramen) would worsen sleep was **not supported**. Participants who ate heavy snacks actually scored slightly higher. When asked why, respondents explained that going to bed hungry made them sleep *worse* — suggesting that going to bed hungry itself reduces sleep quality.

### Sleep Duration
The hypothesis that **8 hours is optimal** was partially supported. However, participants who slept **6 hours or less** showed unexpectedly high sleep scores. Upon reviewing their profiles, most of these individuals attended **early morning prayer services** — an uncontrollable variable attributed to what the researcher described as "God's grace."

### Noise
When total sleep scores (rather than a single metric) were used, the **quiet condition** did produce the highest scores, suggesting the earlier contradictory results contained measurement errors.

### Exercise
Contrary to expectations, participants who **did not exercise** before bed scored higher than those who did.

## Comprehensive Analysis

| Factor | Hypothesis | Result |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Light (dark) | Better sleep | **Not supported** (mood lamp group scored higher) |
| Noise (quiet) | Better sleep | **Supported** in revised analysis |
| Sleep duration (8 hrs) | Optimal | **Partially supported** (6 hr group anomalously high) |
| No eating before bed | Better sleep | **Not supported** (heavy snack group scored higher) |
| No exercise before bed | Worse sleep | **Not supported** (no-exercise group scored higher) |

## Limitations

1. **Uncontrolled variables** — Stress levels, caffeine intake, and other physiological factors could not be measured with the current methods.
2. **Immeasurable factors** — Spiritual or personal variables (e.g., morning prayer) influenced results in ways that cannot be accounted for statistically.
3. **Small sample size** — With only 32 data points, the dataset is insufficient to draw firm conclusions.
4. **Scoring methodology** — The initial scoring system had flaws that were revised mid-study.
